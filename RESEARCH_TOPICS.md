# Master Research Topics — SOP Improvement Library

These are foundational topics in learning science, cognitive psychology, and instructional design that can continuously inform and improve your PT Study SOP. Research any of these when you want to enhance specific aspects of the system.

---

## TIER 1: Core Learning Science (High Impact)

### 1. Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller)
**What it is:** How working memory limitations affect learning; intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Informs how much information to present at once (Gated Platter)
- Guides chunking strategy in Prime phase
- Explains why visual-first landmarks work better than text lists

**Key researchers:** John Sweller, Paul Chandler, Fred Paas

**Search terms:** "cognitive load theory instruction", "intrinsic extraneous germane load", "element interactivity"

---

### 2. Dual Coding Theory (Paivio)
**What it is:** Information encoded both verbally and visually is remembered better than either alone.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Supports drawing protocol effectiveness
- Explains why landmarks + names > names alone
- Informs multi-modal encoding strategies

**Key researchers:** Allan Paivio, Richard Mayer

**Search terms:** "dual coding theory learning", "multimedia learning principles", "verbal visual encoding"

---

### 3. Levels of Processing (Craik & Lockhart)
**What it is:** Deeper semantic processing creates stronger memory traces than shallow processing.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why elaborative interrogation works (Seed-Lock)
- Why function-first beats structure-first
- Why teach-back at L2 is the gate

**Key researchers:** Fergus Craik, Robert Lockhart

**Search terms:** "levels of processing framework", "depth of processing memory", "semantic encoding"

---

### 4. Schema Theory
**What it is:** Knowledge is organized in mental frameworks; new info integrates into existing schemas.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why priming (M2) matters before encoding
- How to structure anatomy mental atlas
- Why connecting to prior knowledge improves retention

**Key researchers:** Frederic Bartlett, Richard Anderson, David Rumelhart

**Search terms:** "schema theory learning", "prior knowledge activation", "knowledge structures"

---

### 5. Testing Effect / Retrieval Practice
**What it is:** Retrieving information strengthens memory more than re-studying.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Core principle behind Sprint mode
- Why Seed-Lock requires production
- Why teach-back is a gate

**Key researchers:** Henry Roediger, Jeffrey Karpicke, Mark McDaniel

**Search terms:** "testing effect learning", "retrieval practice benefits", "test-enhanced learning"

---

## TIER 2: Memory & Encoding (Medium-High Impact)

### 6. Generation Effect
**What it is:** Self-generated information is remembered better than passively received information.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why user MUST supply Seeds
- Why Gated Platter requires editing, not accepting
- Why user-created cards beat AI-created cards

**Key researchers:** Norman Slamecka, Peter Graf

**Search terms:** "generation effect memory", "self-generated learning", "production effect"

---

### 7. Spacing Effect
**What it is:** Distributed practice over time beats massed practice (cramming).

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Informs Brain spacing analytics
- Guides "time since last review" calculations
- Supports session scheduling recommendations

**Key researchers:** Hermann Ebbinghaus, Robert Bjork, Hal Pashler

**Search terms:** "spacing effect learning", "distributed practice", "optimal spacing intervals"

---

### 8. Interleaving
**What it is:** Mixing different topics/skills during practice improves discrimination and transfer.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- How to structure Sprint mode across topics
- Why bucket rotation matters
- Contrast with blocked practice limitations

**Key researchers:** Doug Rohrer, Kelli Taylor, Robert Bjork

**Search terms:** "interleaved practice learning", "interleaving vs blocking", "mixed practice benefits"

---

### 9. Encoding Specificity
**What it is:** Retrieval is easier when conditions match encoding conditions.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why study conditions should match test conditions
- How context affects recall
- Why varied practice locations can help or hurt

**Key researchers:** Endel Tulving, Donald Thomson

**Search terms:** "encoding specificity principle", "context-dependent memory", "transfer appropriate processing"

---

### 10. Elaborative Interrogation
**What it is:** Asking "why" and "how" questions during learning improves retention.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Already core to Seed-Lock mechanism
- Supports function-first approach
- Explains why passive reception fails

**Key researchers:** Michael Pressley, Mark McDaniel

**Search terms:** "elaborative interrogation learning", "why questions encoding", "self-explanation effect"

---

## TIER 3: Metacognition & Self-Regulation (Medium Impact)

### 11. Metacognitive Monitoring & Control
**What it is:** Awareness of one's own learning state and ability to adjust strategies accordingly.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why self-ratings matter
- How to improve calibration
- When to trigger rollback

**Key researchers:** Thomas Nelson, Louis Narens, Gregory Schraw

**Search terms:** "metacognitive monitoring learning", "metacognitive control strategies", "calibration accuracy"

---

### 12. Self-Regulated Learning (SRL)
**What it is:** Learner's ability to plan, monitor, and evaluate their own learning process.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Supports Planning Phase (M0)
- Informs Wrap phase reflection
- Guides Brain dashboard design

**Key researchers:** Barry Zimmerman, Paul Pintrich, Philip Winne

**Search terms:** "self-regulated learning model", "SRL cycle", "forethought performance reflection"

---

### 13. Illusion of Competence
**What it is:** Fluency during study creates false confidence; recognition ≠ recall.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why L2 gate matters (prove understanding)
- Why Sprint mode exposes real gaps
- Why re-reading is ineffective

**Key researchers:** Robert Bjork, Nate Kornell

**Search terms:** "illusion of competence", "fluency illusion learning", "metacognitive illusions"

---

### 14. Hypercorrection Effect
**What it is:** Errors made with high confidence are more likely to be corrected and remembered.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Why Sprint mode failures are valuable
- Why confident wrong answers → strong corrections
- Supports fail-first logic

**Key researchers:** Janet Metcalfe, Nate Kornell

**Search terms:** "hypercorrection effect", "error correction learning", "confident errors memory"

---

## TIER 4: Instructional Design & Teaching (Medium Impact)

### 15. Worked Examples Effect
**What it is:** Studying worked examples is more effective than problem-solving for novices.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- When to show examples vs. demand production
- Balance for different learner stages
- Supports Gated Platter design

**Key researchers:** John Sweller, Alexander Renkl

**Search terms:** "worked examples effect", "example-based learning", "faded worked examples"

---

### 16. Productive Failure
**What it is:** Struggling with problems before instruction can improve learning.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Supports Sprint mode (fail-first)
- Why struggle is desirable
- When to allow failure vs. scaffold

**Key researchers:** Manu Kapur

**Search terms:** "productive failure learning", "invention activities", "problem solving before instruction"

---

### 17. Scaffolding & Fading
**What it is:** Gradually removing support as learner competence increases.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- How to transition Core → Sprint → Drill
- When to reduce Gated Platter assistance
- Level progression design

**Key researchers:** Lev Vygotsky (zone of proximal development), Collins & Kapur

**Search terms:** "scaffolding instruction", "fading scaffolds", "zone of proximal development"

---

### 18. Feedback Timing & Type
**What it is:** When and how feedback is given affects learning outcomes.

**Why it matters for SOP:**
- Immediate vs. delayed feedback in Sprint mode
- Error correction strategies
- Elaborated vs. simple feedback

**Key researchers:** John Hattie, Valerie Shute

**Search terms:** "feedback timing learning", "elaborated feedback", "formative feedback effects"

---

## HOW TO USE THIS LIST

### When to Research:

1. **SOP feels weak in an area** → Find the relevant topic, research it, add to methods library
2. **Building new engine** → Research domain-specific learning science
3. **Brain analytics design** → Research metacognition and calibration
4. **Stuck on mechanism design** → Research foundational principles

### Research Process:

1. Start with Wikipedia or textbook overview
2. Find seminal papers (usually cited thousands of times)
3. Look for recent reviews or meta-analyses
4. Extract actionable principles
5. Update SOP methods library
6. Test in real sessions

### Output Format:

When you research a topic, add to `sop/methods/` with:
- What it means (plain language)
- Why it works (mechanism)
- How it applies to SOP (specific features)
- Source citations
